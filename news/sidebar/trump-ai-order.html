The Silicon Caesar: Trump’s AI Executive Order and the Art of Regulatory Vandalism

By: Edward “The Professor” Miller Dec 12, 2025

Good afternoon, students. Edward “The Professor” Miller reporting for Dumbland News. Today’s lecture explores a fresh masterpiece of American political theater: President Donald Trump’s Executive Order blocking U.S. states from regulating artificial intelligence. A move framed as “innovation,” but executed with all the subtlety of a Roman emperor swinging a golden sledgehammer.

The Report

The President warned that states like California and Colorado were poised to “suffocate” AI innovation with a “cumbersome tangle of onerous regulations.” His argument: in the global AI race, “there’s only going to be one winner,” and China’s unified regulatory model—ethically questionable as it may be—offers a competitive advantage. The horror, he insisted, of companies needing “50 authorizations in 50 states” was simply “impossible.”

Of course, this ignores two centuries of American federalism, where states act as “laboratories of democracy.” Colorado, Utah, and California have already passed laws requiring algorithmic transparency and bias assessments—basic guardrails for technologies that now influence hiring, lending, and healthcare. These are not radical ideas; they are overdue protections.

Yet the Executive Order directs the Attorney General to challenge state AI laws and instructs the Department of Commerce to compile a list of “problematic” regulations. The kicker: states that dare regulate AI risk losing federal funding, including broadband grants. Innovation by coercion—truly the American way.

Venture capitalist David Sacks, whose financial interests in AI are as large as his political influence, reassured the public that only the “most onerous” state rules would be targeted. Child safety laws, he claimed, would remain untouched. Translation: “We’ll keep the guardrails that don’t cost our donors money.”

The timing is also convenient. As rumors swirl about the President’s health and the Epstein files dominate headlines, the administration drops a sweeping policy shift. A classic misdirection: “Look at the shiny AI order while we quietly bury the uncomfortable stuff.”

Ultimately, the order tells states—Colorado, California, Utah, and Texas among them—to step aside and let Silicon Valley handle ethics. It’s a return to Gilded Age logic: what’s good for Google is good for America.

Conclusion

Trump’s Executive Order is less about preparing the U.S. for the future of AI and more about shielding corporations from democratic oversight. By threatening funding and empowering federal challenges to state laws, the administration signals that in the AI era, the government’s priority is not protecting citizens from algorithmic discrimination, but protecting companies from accountability. James Madison would be weeping into his powdered wig.
