Clair Obscur: Expedition 33 Becomes the Most Awarded Game of The Game Awards 2025

By: Anastasia “The Hacker” Ivanova Dec 12, 2025

Good evening, digital wanderers. Anastasia “The Hacker” Ivanova reporting for Dumbland News. While global politics continues crashing like a Windows ME machine running on hopes and prayers, the gaming industry delivered something far more stable: a fully functional masterpiece named Clair Obscur: Expedition 33. With nine awards—including the almighty Game of the Year—this aggressively French RPG didn’t just win; it executed a full system takeover.

The Report

The Game Awards 2025 unfolded on December 11 with its usual cocktail of orchestral drama, celebrity cameos, and enough fog machines to simulate a mid-tier cyberpunk dystopia. But the real spectacle was Clair Obscur: Expedition 33, which swept categories like Best Narrative, Best Direction, Best Art Direction, Best Score, Best Indie Debut, and Best RPG. If this were a software environment, we’d call it a privilege escalation exploit—one that lets a relatively small studio dominate the entire awards ecosystem while AAA giants stare blankly like outdated antivirus programs.

Developed by Sandfall Interactive and published by Kepler Interactive, the game outperformed heavyweights such as Death Stranding 2: On the Beach, Hades II, Hollow Knight: Silksong, Kingdom Come: Deliverance II, and even the chaotic fever dream known as Donkey Kong Bananza. (Yes, that’s the real title. No, I don’t know what happened at Nintendo. Possibly a banana-related memory leak.)

With its atmospheric worldbuilding, haunting score by Lorien Testard, and a standout performance by Jennifer English, the game became the awards season’s equivalent of a perfectly optimized engine—smooth, elegant, and blissfully free of the microtransaction bloat that certain right-wing politicians would call “free market innovation.” Meanwhile, the left would probably argue the awards should be redistributed among the nominees. But for once, meritocracy actually worked. Shocking, I know.

Interestingly, the Players’ Voice award went to Wuthering Waves, proving once again that gamers, like voters, sometimes choose differently from the experts—though at least in this case, nobody tried to overturn the results via a poorly organized online coup.

Other winners included No Man’s Sky for Best Ongoing Game (a redemption arc so strong it should be taught in political science courses), Umamusume: Pretty Derby for Best Mobile Game, The Midnight Walk for Best VR/AR, and Fatal Fury: City of Wolves for Best Fighting Game. Hades II took Best Action Game, while Hollow Knight: Silksong—a title so anticipated it has become a meme—secured Best Action/Adventure.

On the esports side, Counter-Strike 2 won Best Esports Game, with Chovy crowned Best Esports Athlete and Team Vitality taking Best Esports Team. MoistCr1TiKaL earned Content Creator of the Year, proving that speaking very fast into a microphone remains a viable career path.

Meanwhile, Grand Theft Auto VI won Most Anticipated Game, which is essentially the awards-show equivalent of giving a trophy to a baby for “Most Likely to Crash the Internet Upon Release.” And Doom: The Dark Ages received the Innovation in Accessibility award, proving that even a franchise built on demon-slaying carnage can still care about user experience—something many governments still treat as optional DLC.

Conclusion

With its record-breaking nine awards, Clair Obscur: Expedition 33 didn’t just dominate The Game Awards 2025—it redefined what a modern RPG can achieve when artistry, innovation, and technical precision align. In a world where political systems crash more often than early-2000s Windows, it’s refreshing to see a complex, ambitious project actually run without catastrophic failure. If only global governance could take notes from Sandfall Interactive’s development pipeline.
