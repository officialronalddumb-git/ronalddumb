The Report

Mexico’s labor reform — a gradual reduction from 48 to 40 hours by 2030 — has been hailed as a win for workers, unions, and anyone who has ever fantasized about a weekend that doesn’t feel like a loading screen. But while the world leans toward balance, Elon Musk is out here reminding everyone that he built his empire by working “almost every hour of the day.” Somewhere, a tired office worker just whispered: “Sir… please.”

Musk’s now‑viral comments resurfaced after Tesla shareholders approved a compensation package so massive it could make him the world’s first trillionaire. And nothing says “relatable advice” like a man worth $491 billion telling graduates that the secret to success is grinding 100 hours a week. It’s the ultimate “rise and grind” meme, except he’s dead serious.

During a speech at USC’s Marshall School of Business, Musk encouraged young people to “work very hard,” adding that if someone works 50 hours and you work 100, you’ll accomplish twice as much. “Simple math,” he said — instantly becoming the internet’s favorite new meme template:

Musk also revisited his origin story: he and his brother Kimbal running their first company with “only one computer,” taking turns sleeping in the office like it was a Silicon Valley version of musical chairs. Inspirational? Maybe. A red flag for labor advocates? Absolutely.

Critics argue that Musk’s philosophy glamorizes burnout and ignores the human cost. Diana Hussein of United Auto Workers famously clapped back in 2018, saying that the 40‑hour week did change the world — by preventing corporations from treating workers like NPCs in a never‑ending side quest. Meanwhile, Musk’s defenders insist he’s simply promoting ambition, not exploitation. The debate is basically a meme war at this point.

And then there’s the cautionary tale of Esther Crawford, the Twitter product director who literally slept in the office to meet Musk’s deadlines — only to be laid off later. If Aesop were alive today, he’d write: “The woman who slept at work learned that loyalty is not a mattress.”

Beyond the memes, the EU’s sanctions on X have added political spice to the mix, with U.S. officials accusing Brussels of targeting tech companies. The EU responded with the diplomatic equivalent of “cope harder,” insisting it’s simply enforcing its laws. Meanwhile, Musk’s comments continue to shape work culture in tech and manufacturing, where “hustle” is often treated like a religion.

So the real question becomes: does working more guarantee success? Or is it just another episode of “Billionaires Explain Life to People Who Don’t Own Rockets”?

Conclusion

Elon Musk’s rejection of the 40‑hour workweek reignites a global debate about productivity, dignity, and the myth of the heroic overworker. While his personal story is undeniably impressive, it exists in a universe far removed from the realities of most workers. As nations move toward shorter workweeks, society must decide whether the future looks like a balanced life — or a never‑ending grindset montage set to motivational dubstep.

One thing remains certain: if Musk insists that “simple math” proves 100‑hour weeks are the path to success, millions of workers might respond with an equally simple equation: More life > more hours.
